Not even a pandemic can stop Ghana’s rising death toll from road crashes

Story by Kwasi Gyamfi Asiedu

Last May, authorities were hopeful that Ghana’s high death toll from road crashes would drop significantly. Pedestrian knockdowns were down by 25 percent and the deaths had reduced by 14 percent compared to the first five months of 2019. Road safety campaigners and journalists hoped that lockdown and other pandemic-related measures would lead to the sharpest decline in road deaths in three decades. “I was optimistic that …the trend would have continued and we would have recorded a less number of accidents, fatalities and injuries for the whole year,” says Elvis Darko, editor of The Finder newspaper in Ghana which has consistently reported on road safety issues in Ghana. But the optimism was short lived. As Ghana emerged out of its lockdown and first wave of restrictions — after the economy had been battered by the pandemic — crashes and deaths not only went up, they surpassed 2019's figures. According to recently released data from the Ghana Police Service and National Road Safety Authority, there were 2,589 road accident deaths in Ghana representing a 13.3 percent increase compared to 2,284 deaths in 2019.

Ghana is not the only place to have seen a rise in the number of road fatalities even though we are staying at home often. In Texas, whose total population of 30 million is similar to Ghana’s, there were nearly 3,900 deaths on the road in 2020 compared to the about 3,600 deaths recorded in 2019. This is despite the state’s Department of Transportation recording nearly 50 percent drop in traffic levels on many of its highways. Even in Kenya in East Africa, where pandemic restrictions were longer and tougher, including a strictly enforced nighttime curfew, there was a rise in road deaths in 2020.

An analysis of official data by Columbia News Service shows a rise in the number of road accidents involving motorcycles and a corresponding rise in the number of deaths from motorcycle crashes, becoming the latest public health concern especially in urban areas. Motorcycle accidents in Ghana have increased by 63% since 2017.

Experts say the rise in accidents could be because of Ghana’s relatively short lockdown. However, in 2020, Ghana held general elections and the rigorous travel that comes with campaigning during elections in Ghana may account for the increase despite the pandemic’s restrictions. An analysis of the data suggests an increase in deaths during election years in Ghana.
